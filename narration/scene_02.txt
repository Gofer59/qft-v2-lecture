## Scene 02 — What a Field Actually Is

The word "field" appears in physics in many contexts, and it is worth being precise about what it means before we start quantizing anything.

A field is an assignment of a physical quantity to every point in space — or more generally, to every point in spacetime. At each point, there is a value. That is all.

The simplest example is a temperature field. Imagine you are standing in a large room, and at every point in that room, there is a temperature. Not a temperature attached to any particular object, but a temperature at that location. If you mapped out the temperature everywhere simultaneously, you would have a scalar field — one number per spatial point.

Now imagine the wind. At every point in the atmosphere, there is a velocity — a direction and a magnitude. That is a vector field. Three numbers per point instead of one.

These classical fields are defined by their values, and they change in time. The temperature field obeys a diffusion equation. The wind velocity obeys the Navier-Stokes equations. The dynamics of the field are governed by some equation of motion, derived from a physical principle.

Classical electromagnetism is built on fields too. The electric field and the magnetic field together form a tensor field — at each point in spacetime, you have six numbers encoding the electromagnetic configuration. Maxwell's equations are the equations of motion for this field.

Notice something about all of these classical field examples. None of them is attached to a particular object. The temperature at a point in the room does not belong to any particular air molecule. It is a property of the location. The field exists independently, at every point, whether or not any matter is present. The field is the fundamental thing.

Now here is the conceptual leap. In quantum field theory, the field is still this assignment of a quantity to every spacetime point. But the quantity assigned is no longer a simple number. It is an operator — a mathematical object that acts on quantum states.

A classical field at a point is a number. A quantum field at a point is an operator.

This difference, simple to state, carries an enormous amount of physics. Operators in quantum mechanics do not have definite values — they have expectation values and fluctuations. The quantum field fluctuates at every point in spacetime, even in the state of lowest energy.

There is also a question of what type of quantity the field assigns to each point. A scalar field assigns a single number — one degree of freedom per spacetime location. A vector field assigns a direction and magnitude — four numbers in spacetime. A tensor field assigns a more complex object, like a matrix, at each point. And a spinor field, which we will meet when we discuss the electron, assigns a four-component object with very specific transformation properties under rotations and boosts.

The type of field determines the type of particle. Scalar fields give spin-zero particles. Vector fields give spin-one particles. Spinor fields give spin-one-half particles. The entire particle content of the Standard Model is encoded in the types of quantum fields the theory contains.
